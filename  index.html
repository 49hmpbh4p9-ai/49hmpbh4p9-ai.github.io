Hormuz Crisis — Operational Update

Iran's selective passage and the Yuan gambit: what the intelligence actually shows

As commercial transit collapses to near-zero, Tehran is differentiating between allies and adversaries — and proposing to restructure the petrodollar system in the process

Global Pulse Intelligence Desk | 16 March 2026, 06:00 GMT | Updated: 09:30 GMT
Correction & Update

A previous version of this brief incorrectly stated that Iran's proposed alternative settlement currency was the Japanese Yen. All verified sources confirm the currency is the Chinese Yuan (renminbi). The article has been corrected and re-fact-checked against live reporting as of 09:30 GMT. Updated information on India's passage arrangements and the status of the Yuan proposal has been incorporated throughout.

The Strait of Hormuz — 21 miles wide at its narrowest point and the conduit for roughly 20% of globally traded oil — has not been militarily "closed" in the formal sense. What has happened is arguably more strategically consequential: it has been selectively opened, with access determined by geopolitical alignment rather than commercial right. The distinction matters enormously for manufacturers, insurers, and governments attempting to plan around this crisis.

The Five Eyes alliance, coordinating through the Joint Crisis Operations Intelligence Center, maintains that Iran's threat posture remains fundamentally economic. The core premise — the "Economic Rationalism" thesis — holds that Iran cannot afford to fully close the Strait because China, its only remaining major superpower patron, depends on Gulf oil transiting that corridor. The new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei has pledged to maintain the closure "as long as the US-Israeli war against Iran continues" — though he added that "the matter may change according to interests," language that Western analysts characterise as preserving an exit ramp. [CNBC]

Regional analysts and private maritime security firms contest this view directly. US forces have sunk 16 Iranian minelayers as reports indicate Tehran was mining the waterway. Ten vessels in or near the Strait came under Iranian attack within the first two weeks of the war, killing at least seven seafarers, according to the International Maritime Organization. These are not the actions of a government engaged purely in theatrical posturing.

"Get ready for oil to be $200 a barrel, because the oil price depends on regional security, which you have destabilised."

Ebrahim Zolfaqari, spokesperson for Iran's militia forces — quoted by CNBC, 13 March 2026

Selective passage: China and India

The picture emerging from verified reporting is more granular — and more strategically deliberate — than a simple open/closed binary. Iran has sent at least 11.7 million barrels of crude oil through the Strait since the war began on 28 February, all destined for China. [CNBC] China is simultaneously in active talks with Iran to allow crude and Qatari LNG vessels safe passage, with three diplomatic sources confirming to Reuters that Beijing is pressing Tehran to formally extend this arrangement. [Jerusalem Post / Reuters]

Iran's government has stated that no vessels belonging to the United States, Israel, European countries, or their allies would be allowed to pass — but the statement made no mention of China. Ship-tracking data has confirmed a vessel called the Iron Maiden passed through the Strait after changing its signalling to "China-owner." [Jerusalem Post]

India has also secured limited bilateral access. Iran's Ambassador to India confirmed that Tehran had allowed some Indian vessels to pass following diplomatic engagement between New Delhi and Tehran. Two Indian-flagged LPG tankers bound for ports on India's west coast crossed the Strait safely. [Al Jazeera] However, 22 Indian-flagged vessels remain west of the waterway, indicating the exemption is selective, not blanket.

The Yuan proposal: confirmed, contested, and consequential

A senior Iranian official told CNN that Tehran is considering allowing a limited number of oil tankers to pass through the Strait on the condition that their cargo is priced in Chinese Yuan. The potential move is described as part of a plan to manage a controlled reopening of the waterway. [IranWire / CNN]

This proposal remains at the consideration stage, not a concluded agreement. Observers in China have reacted cautiously, citing operational feasibility concerns and warning that implementation could strain China–US relations. [South China Morning Post]

The financial implications deserve more analytical attention than they have received in mainstream coverage. If implemented, Iran's proposal would extend Yuan settlement to the world's single most critical maritime chokepoint — a direct challenge to the petrodollar system that has governed global energy trade since 1974. Countries importing oil through Hormuz would need Yuan reserves, not just dollar reserves, quietly shifting global demand from dollar to Yuan without any dramatic formal announcement. China's CIPS payment system has expanded significantly and Yuan-denominated oil trades already occur with Russian and Iranian crude, meaning the infrastructure to execute such an arrangement already exists. [European Business Magazine]

Revised strategic assessment

The Hormuz crisis is no longer primarily a physical infrastructure problem with financial consequences. It has become a geopolitical restructuring event, in which the architecture of global energy trade — its routes, its currencies, and its bilateral arrangements — is being redrawn under pressure. Manufacturers who plan for a return to the pre-crisis status quo are likely planning for a world that no longer exists.

For manufacturers operating outside Chinese and Indian bilateral corridors, the shipping risk remains High with no near-term multilateral resolution in view. Trump's appeal to France, Japan, South Korea, and the United Kingdom to send warships to keep the Strait open has produced no firm commitments. The currency dimension is the most structurally novel element: even at the consideration stage, the Yuan proposal signals that Iran intends to use the Strait as a financial restructuring instrument. Procurement and treasury teams should begin modelling Yuan exposure in energy cost bases now, regardless of whether the proposal is formalised.
